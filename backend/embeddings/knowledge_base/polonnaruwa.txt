Polonnaruwa is the second ancient capital of Sri Lanka and a UNESCO World Heritage Site, designated in 1982. It is located in the North Central Province of Sri Lanka.

The city served as the capital of the Kingdom of Polonnaruwa from the 10th to the 13th century AD. It flourished particularly under King Parakramabahu I (1153–1186 AD), who unified the entire island under a single ruler for the first time.

Key monuments at Polonnaruwa include the Gal Vihara, a rock temple with four magnificent Buddha statues carved out of a single granite face. The statues include a seated meditating Buddha, a standing Buddha, and a colossal reclining Buddha measuring 14 meters in length.

The Parakrama Samudra (Sea of Parakrama) is an ancient reservoir created by King Parakramabahu with a perimeter of over 14 km. It remains one of the largest ancient man-made reservoirs in the world and is still in use today for irrigation.

Other notable ruins include the Vatadage (a circular relic house), the Rankoth Vehera stupa, the Lankatilaka image house, and the Royal Palace complex.

The Archaeological Museum at Polonnaruwa provides detailed exhibits on the history, architectural achievements, and artifacts of the Polonnaruwa Kingdom.

Polonnaruwa is a key component of the Cultural Triangle of Sri Lanka along with Anuradhapura and Kandy, and attracts thousands of visitors for its well-preserved ancient ruins and historical significance.
