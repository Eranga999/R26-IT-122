Sigiriya Rock Fortress is one of the most iconic heritage sites in Sri Lanka and a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1982.

Located in the Matale District of central Sri Lanka, it rises approximately 200 meters above the flat plains. The site was built by King Kashyapa I between 477 and 495 AD.

The rock summit contains the ruins of an ancient palace complex. The western face of the rock contains the famous Sigiriya frescoes depicting celestial maidens and a mirror wall inscribed with ancient graffiti verse.

The lion staircase, named after the large lion paws carved at the base of the summit stairway, is a signature feature of the site. The archaeological site includes elaborate water gardens, boulder gardens, and terraced gardens considered among the oldest landscaped gardens in the world.

Sigiriya is referred to in ancient Sri Lankan chronicles such as the Culavamsa. It serves as one of the best preserved examples of ancient urban planning in South Asia.

The site attracts over half a million tourists annually and is the most visited heritage attraction in Sri Lanka.

Conservation efforts are ongoing by the Central Cultural Fund of Sri Lanka in partnership with UNESCO and international archaeologists.
