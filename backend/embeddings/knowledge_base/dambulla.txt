The Dambulla Cave Temple, also known as the Golden Temple of Dambulla, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site located in Dambulla, Sri Lanka.

It is the largest and best-preserved cave temple complex in Sri Lanka. The site dates back to the first century BC, when King Valagamba of Anuradhapura took refuge in the caves after being exiled.

The complex consists of five caves, each containing Buddhist shrines and statues. In total, the complex houses 153 Buddha statues, 3 statues of Sri Lankan kings, and 4 statues of gods and goddesses.

The caves are adorned with paintings covering an area of 2,100 square meters, depicting the life of Gautama Buddha and various episodes from Sri Lankan history and mythology.

The site is an active place of worship and has been a pilgrimage destination for over two thousand years. It was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1991.

The Golden Temple Museum at the entrance features a large golden Buddha statue and a museum showcasing the history of the site.

Dambulla is situated in the central province of Sri Lanka, approximately 148 km north-east of Colombo. It is a key stop on the Cultural Triangle heritage route that also includes Sigiriya, Polonnaruwa, and Kandy.
